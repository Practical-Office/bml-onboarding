Introduction — why Build Loop

~25 min · playbook

Section 1

Why this matters

Most teams can ship software. The harder question — especially for new products and major bets — is whether anyone wants what you built enough to sustain a business around it. That question lives in extreme uncertainty. You do not know yet if the problem is real, if your solution fits, or if customers will pay, adopt, or return.

Under uncertainty, shipping more features is not the same as making progress. A team can close dozens of tickets, hit sprint velocity targets, and still learn nothing decisive about the bet they are running. Activity looks productive; the business model stays unproven.

Validated learning is the unit of progress that matters here: evidence, from real-world contact with customers or users, that a belief about value, growth, or feasibility is true or false. Not opinions in a roadmap doc. Not internal applause for a launch. Learning you can point to and act on.

Build Loop Mastery exists because Practical AI teams need a shared experiment system — not a feature factory with prettier standups. The Build–Measure–Learn Loop is that system: turn an idea into something testable, measure how people respond, then decide whether to continue, change course, or stop. Faster cycles through that loop beat longer cycles through a backlog of unvalidated assumptions.

This module frames the mindset. Later modules show you how to run experiments on GitHub, write tickets, and operate the board. Start here so every experiment you run has a clear why.

Section 2

Ideas / concepts

Uncertainty is the default

New products and significant pivots are not execution problems alone. They are learning problems. The team must discover whether the world behaves the way their strategy assumes. Until that discovery happens, roadmaps are hypotheses written in calendar form.

Two questions sit above “Can we build it?”:

  • Should we build this? — Is there a problem worth solving, for people who will engage?
  • Can we build a sustainable business around it? — Will behavior, willingness to pay, or growth mechanics support the vision?

Build Loop work makes those questions explicit instead of burying them under feature lists.

Validated learning as progress

Traditional project management measures progress by outputs: stories closed, milestones hit, releases shipped. Under uncertainty, outputs can increase while knowledge stays flat.

Validated learning means you ran something in the real world and updated your beliefs with evidence. Examples of validated learning (concept-level):

  • Users who saw the new flow completed a key action at twice the baseline rate.
  • Prospects who received the offer declined uniformly — the value hypothesis failed fast.
  • A manual concierge version of the service retained fewer than one in ten participants — the problem may be weaker than assumed.

Invalidated learning also counts: you learned the assumption was wrong. That is progress if you record it and change behavior. Shipping without measuring, or measuring without deciding, is not progress — it is theater.

Risky assumptions (leap-of-faith)

Every product bet rests on assumptions. Some are safe; others, if wrong, collapse the whole strategy. Those are risky assumptions — sometimes called leap-of-faith assumptions in Lean Startup vocabulary.

Common categories:

  • Value — Users will find this useful enough to change behavior.
  • Growth — Acquired users will invite others, return, or expand usage without unsustainable spend.
  • Feasibility — We can deliver the experience at acceptable cost and quality.
  • Channel / willingness to pay — Customers will pay, or adopt through the channel we can reach.

Build Loop experiments should name the riskiest assumption up front. The ticket is a hypothesis, not a feature wishlist with a deadline.

Why not a feature factory?

A feature factory optimizes for throughput: more stories, more releases, more surface area. Inputs and outputs are visible; learning is optional. Symptoms include:

  • Backlogs grow faster than validated conclusions.
  • “Done” means merged, not learned.
  • Metrics are reported but rarely change the next sprint.
  • Learn columns stay empty or hold vague retrospectives with no persevere / pivot / kill decision.

Feature factories feel busy. Build Loop teams feel decisive — even when the decision is to stop.

The Build–Measure–Learn Loop (introduction)

The fundamental activity, attributed to Eric Ries’s Lean Startup methodology, is a repeating cycle:

  1. Build — Create the smallest version that can test the risky assumption (often called a minimum viable product, or MVP).
  2. Measure — Observe how customers or users respond using metrics chosen before you ship.
  3. Learn — Interpret results, write what you learned, and decide: persevere on the current path, pivot to a new hypothesis, or kill the bet.

Practical AI adds an explicit kill outcome so teams stop experiments cleanly instead of letting failed bets linger as zombie projects.

Optimize for cycle time: how fast you move from idea to decision. A small experiment decided in a week beats a large release debated for a quarter.

MVP — preview for Module 2

A minimum viable product is the simplest build that starts learning about core assumptions with real users — not a half-finished product shipped for its own sake. The MVP is sized for the hypothesis, not the roadmap. Module 2 goes deeper on phases, exit criteria, and metrics.

Section 3

How to do it

Before you open a repo or file a ticket, run this sequence mentally (or in a short doc). It takes minutes and prevents feature-factory drift.

  1. Name the bet. State in one sentence what you are trying to learn — not what you are trying to ship. Example shape: “We believe [segment] will [behavior] because [reason].”
  2. Pick the riskiest assumption. List two or three assumptions; circle the one that would kill the bet if false. That assumption drives the experiment.
  3. Define what would falsify it. Write what evidence would mean “this assumption is wrong” before you build. Without falsification criteria, any result can be spun as success.
  4. Size the Build for learning. Ask: what is the smallest thing we can put in front of real users this cycle? Cut scope until the answer tests the assumption, not the whole vision.
  5. Pre-register metrics. Choose one or two measures that tie directly to the assumption. Decide pass / kill thresholds in advance (Module 2 covers actionable vs vanity metrics).
  6. Plan the Learn decision. Agree who records the outcome and which labels apply: persevere, pivot, or kill. No “we’ll see” without a decision owner.
  7. Start the loop. Move the work through Build → Measure → Learn. When Learn is done, the backlog gets the next hypothesis — not an automatic continuation of the old plan.

If you cannot complete steps 2–5, you are not ready to Build yet. You are still in ideation — which is fine, but do not pretend it is an experiment.

Section 4

How-to micro-scenario

Abstract walkthrough — no product names, no book case studies.

A small team serves remote professionals who struggle to coordinate recurring group activities across time zones. Leadership wants a full scheduling product with integrations, mobile apps, and AI suggestions. The team pushes back: nobody has validated that professionals will trust a new tool for this job.

Bet stated: “We believe busy remote workers will complete a group scheduling task using a lightweight web flow if it saves them more than ten minutes compared to email threads.”

Riskiest assumption: Value — users will finish scheduling through an unfamiliar interface instead of reverting to email.

Falsification: Fewer than 40% of invited participants complete scheduling within 48 hours.

Build (MVP-sized): A single web page: organizer picks three time windows, participants tap one option, organizer sees confirmations. No accounts, no calendar sync, no native apps. Built in days, not months.

Measure: Completion rate per invite; time from first link open to confirmed slot; optional one-question survey on “would you use this again?”

Learn: After two organizer cohorts (n ≈ 30 invites), completion hits 62%. Persevere on the web-first flow; defer mobile and integrations. Document: email escape hatches dropped when reminders were added — hypothesis for next loop.

Notice what did not happen: they did not ship a feature factory quarter. They did not count page views as victory. They wrote a decision and fed the next experiment.

Failure modes

  • Shipping as progress. Celebrating merge or launch without pre-defined learning criteria. Fix: no Build ticket closes until Measure and Learn owners are named.
  • Vague hypotheses. “Improve onboarding” is a theme, not a testable belief. Fix: one sentence with segment, behavior, and expected change.
  • Testing the safe assumption. Experiments that only validate easy technical work while ignoring value or growth risk. Fix: always label the riskiest assumption on the ticket.
  • Immune metrics. Choosing measures that cannot fail (total signups, raw page views). Fix: prefer rates and behaviors tied to the assumption (Module 2).
  • Learn theater. Retrospectives that never produce persevere, pivot, or kill. Fix: Learn column requires a decision label before Done.
  • Oversized MVP. Building the roadmap slice “because we’ll need it anyway.” Fix: cut until the build only serves the current hypothesis.
  • Ignoring kill. Letting failed bets continue as low-priority maintenance. Fix: kill explicitly; archive learning so the board stays honest.

1. Shipping vs validated learning

Shipping adds outputs to the world; validated learning updates beliefs with evidence from how people respond. You can ship without learning (launch nobody uses). You can learn without a large ship (concierge test, prototype, smoke test). Under uncertainty, learning is the progress that reduces risk on the business bet.

2. Riskiest assumption first

If the riskiest assumption is false, the rest of the strategy does not matter — polishing secondary features wastes time. Testing it early maximizes learning per unit of effort and avoids building on a false foundation.

3. Feature factory signs
  1. Velocity and output metrics dominate; learning metrics are absent or ignored.
  2. Work is “done” at merge without a Measure / Learn record.
  3. Backlog grows from opinions, not from prior experiment decisions.
4. Cycle time

Cycle time is the elapsed time from hypothesis to decision. Shorter cycles mean more experiments per quarter, faster correction when wrong, and less sunk cost on false paths. The loop’s power is speed through Build → Measure → Learn, not size of any single Build.

The Build–Measure–Learn Loop

~25 min · playbook

Section 1

Why this matters

Module 1 argued that validated learning beats feature velocity under uncertainty. Module 2 gives you the operating mechanics of the Build–Measure–Learn Loop — what each phase is for, when you are allowed to leave it, and how Learn produces real decisions.

Teams that skip phase discipline drift back into theater: big Builds with fuzzy Measure plans, dashboards full of numbers that never change priorities, and Learn columns that hold narrative without persevere, pivot, or kill. The loop looks right on a board; behavior stays factory-shaped.

Getting the phases right is how Build Loop Mastery stays portable across products. The columns are the same whether you are testing pricing, onboarding, or a new agent workflow: Build tests a hypothesis, Measure gathers evidence against pre-registered criteria, Learn commits to a next move. Practical AI’s explicit kill path prevents zombie experiments that drain attention after the hypothesis has already failed.

Section 2

Ideas / concepts

Three phases and exit criteria

Each phase has a job and a gate — criteria for moving forward. Without gates, work slides through the board on momentum instead of evidence.

PhasePurposeExit criteria (examples)
Backlog Hold experiment ideas with a named hypothesis and riskiest assumption. Ticket states falsification criteria and owner; ready to enter Build.
Build Produce the MVP — smallest build that starts learning with real users. Testable artifact deployed or delivered to a defined cohort; instrumentation in place.
Measure Run the observation window; collect actionable metrics against thresholds. Pre-defined time window or sample size reached; data sufficient to compare to pass/kill.

If Learn rarely changes what you do next, the loop is broken — regardless of how polished the Kanban looks.

MVP as learning vehicle

The minimum viable product is not a compromise product for impatient stakeholders. It is the smallest build that lets the team start learning about the core assumption with real users, at the least effort that still produces credible evidence.

Good MVP instincts:

  • Scope to the hypothesis, not the multi-year roadmap.
  • Prefer formats that test value early: concierge, wizard-of-oz, single-flow prototype, limited cohort — when full automation is not what you are learning about yet.
  • Accept rough edges where they do not contaminate the metric (ugly UI is fine if you are testing willingness to complete a task).
  • Reject “we need the whole platform first” unless feasibility is the riskiest assumption.

An MVP that cannot falsify the riskiest assumption is oversized. An MVP that tests multiple unrelated assumptions at once muddies Measure. One loop, one primary assumption.

Actionable vs vanity metrics

Vanity metrics look good in updates but do not tell you what to do next. Totals that only go up (registered users, cumulative downloads, raw page views) often hide flat engagement or weak retention. They are easy to report and hard to act on.

Actionable metrics support a decision and show a plausible cause–effect link to the behavior you changed. They are tied to the hypothesis, measured per cohort or per period, and compared to thresholds you set before Build.

Vanity (avoid as primary)Actionable (prefer)
Total signups everActivation rate within 7 days for this cohort
Page views on launch day% who completed the core action after seeing the new flow
Number of features shippedRepeat usage rate among users who tried the feature once
Social impressionsConversion from invite to confirmed participant

Innovation accounting (light touch): treat each experiment as a accounting period for learning. Establish a baseline from the MVP, tune the engine with subsequent loops, then decide. You do not need the full textbook system in this module — you need baselines, thresholds, and honest comparison.

Persevere, pivot, and kill

After Measure, Learn must produce a structured decision. Eric Ries’s public methodology emphasizes pivot or persevere. Practical AI adds kill as an explicit third outcome for operational clarity.

  • Persevere — Evidence supports the current hypothesis and drivers are moving in the right direction. Continue investing along this strategy; next Build deepens or extends what worked.
  • Pivot — A structured course correction: change strategy, segment, channel, or core hypothesis while keeping the broader vision. One foot anchored, one foot moves. Next ticket states the new risky assumption.
  • Kill (Practical AI extension) — Stop the experiment or drop the bet. Criteria show the hypothesis failed or the opportunity is too weak to justify more cycles. Archive learning so the team does not revive dead bets through inertia. Kill is not failure of the team; it is success of the system.

Pivot is not a euphemism for kill. Pivot keeps a version of the vision alive with a new testable path. Kill ends the line of inquiry unless leadership explicitly reopens it with new evidence.

Section 3

How to do it

Use this sequence for every experiment ticket. It mirrors the board columns and keeps Measure honest.

  1. Backlog — write the hypothesis block. One sentence bet, riskiest assumption labeled, falsification statement (“We will consider the hypothesis false if…”).
  2. Backlog — pre-register metrics. Pick one or two actionable metrics. Write pass and kill thresholds numerically. Avoid metrics you cannot influence with this Build.
  3. Build — cut to MVP. List everything the roadmap wants; delete anything that does not serve the current assumption. Ship to a defined cohort or environment.
  4. Build — instrument before launch. Events, funnel steps, or manual tally sheet — whatever matches your maturity. No instrument, no move to Measure.
  5. Measure — run the window. Do not peek for early celebration. Use the sample size or time box you committed to unless there is a safety stop.
  6. Measure — compare to thresholds. Pass, fail, or ambiguous against pre-registered criteria — not against hopes.
  7. Learn — write validated learning. Short prose: what we believed, what happened, what we now believe. Link charts or tables.
  8. Learn — label the decision. Apply persevere, pivot, or kill. Create the follow-up ticket (next hypothesis) or archive with reason.
  9. Done — close the loop. Ensure the board reflects reality; do not start the next oversized Build until the decision is recorded.

Section 4

How-to micro-scenario

Abstract walkthrough — no product names, no book case studies.

A team offers a digital checklist tool for volunteer coordinators. Usage is flat. A PM proposes adding gamification — badges, streaks, leaderboards — to “make it fun.” A skeptical engineer asks what assumption that tests.

Hypothesis: “Coordinators who see progress badges will return to the tool within seven days at a higher rate than coordinators who do not.”

Riskiest assumption: Value through motivational design — engagement is low because the experience feels unrewarding, not because the checklist itself is wrong.

MVP: Badge on dashboard for “week fully planned,” shown to 50% of active accounts via feature flag. No leaderboard, no streaks — those are a second loop if badges move the metric.

Actionable metric: Seven-day return rate among coordinators who completed at least one checklist in the prior month, badge cohort vs holdout.

Pass / kill: Pass if badge cohort return rate is ≥ 15% relative lift; kill gamification path if lift < 5% after four weeks.

Measure: Window closes at four weeks; badge cohort shows 6% lift — within noise.

Learn — kill this hypothesis path: “Badges did not materially change return behavior. Low engagement likely sits earlier in the workflow (setup friction), not motivational chrome.” Follow-up ticket pivots to testing a one-screen setup wizard — new risky assumption documented.

Vanity metric avoided: “10,000 badges displayed” would have looked triumphant while return rate barely moved.

Failure modes

  • Phase blur. Merging Build and Measure without a shippable gate — debating data before anything reaches users. Fix: explicit Build exit checklist.
  • Moving goalposts. Changing pass/kill thresholds after results arrive. Fix: thresholds live in the ticket before Build; changes require a new experiment ID.
  • Vanity dashboard. Reporting cumulative totals that always rise. Fix: cohort rates, period comparisons, behavior tied to hypothesis.
  • Omnibus MVP. One release testing pricing, onboarding, and referrals simultaneously. Fix: split loops; one primary assumption each.
  • Learn without labels. Long narrative, no persevere / pivot / kill. Fix: decision label required to leave Learn.
  • Pivot sprawl. “Pivot” that changes everything at once — no anchor, no new falsifiable ticket. Fix: state what stays, what changes, and the new riskiest assumption.
  • Soft kill. Deprioritizing without recording kill — bet revives in the next planning cycle. Fix: kill path archives learning and removes from active backlog.
  • Infinite persevere. Continuing a strategy despite flat or negative drivers because “we already invested.” Fix: pre-register what “no signal” means and when to pivot or kill.

Section 7

Self-check

Answer in your head first, then expand each item.

  1. What must be true before a ticket moves from Build to Measure?
  2. How is an MVP different from a minimal feature release?
  3. Give an example of turning a vanity metric into an actionable one.
  4. When is kill better than pivot?
1. Build → Measure gate

The team must have a shippable (or deliverable) test artifact in front of a defined audience, with instrumentation or a data collection plan ready. Without that, you are not measuring customer response — you are still building. Exit criteria should be verifiable: deployed flag, cohort invited, tally sheet assigned, etc.

2. MVP vs minimal release

A minimal release ships the next roadmap slice early. An MVP ships the smallest thing that tests a specific learning goal. An MVP can be manual, ugly, or narrow if that is what falsifies the assumption. A minimal release optimizes for product completeness; an MVP optimizes for learning per effort.

3. Vanity → actionable

Vanity: “5,000 total accounts created.” Actionable: “Of accounts created in this experiment’s two-week window, what percentage added a second item within 72 hours?” The second metric ties to behavior after the change you introduced and supports a persevere / pivot / kill call.

4. Kill vs pivot

Kill when the hypothesis failed against pre-registered criteria and there is no credible adjacent assumption worth testing under the same vision — or when the opportunity is too small to justify more cycles. Pivot when evidence refutes one specific assumption but a related strategy (new segment, channel, or problem slice) remains plausible and testable. Kill stops theater; pivot redirects energy with a new falsifiable ticket.

Module 3 · ~20 min

GitHub Setup

Issues hold the truth; the Project board is the view. Learn which labels matter, why we skip phase labels, and how Stage columns carry the loop.

3

Why

One experiment, one issue — visible on a board

~20 minutes

Validated learning needs a durable record and a shared picture of where work sits in the loop. GitHub Issues give you the record: hypothesis, build plan, metrics, and learn decision live on one ticket. A GitHub Project gives you the picture: columns show whether an experiment is waiting, building, measuring, deciding, or done.

Splitting those roles keeps creation fast and prevents drift. If you duplicate phase information in both columns and labels, people update one and forget the other. The board columns own the phase; labels mark what kind of ticket it is and what you decided at Learn — nothing more.

Rule of thumb: If you cannot answer “where is this experiment in the loop?” from the Project board alone, your setup is too complicated.

How-to

One-time setup with gh

1. Verify labels

gh label list | rg 'experiment|persevere|pivot|kill-candidate'

If any are missing, create them:

gh label create experiment --description "Build Loop experiment ticket" --color 0E8A16
gh label create persevere --description "Learn decision — keep going" --color 0E8A16
gh label create pivot --description "Learn decision — change approach" --color FBCA04
gh label create kill-candidate --description "Learn decision — kill or sunset" --color D93F0B

2. Create the Project board

From the repo root, run the project script (requires gh auth refresh -s project,read:project first):

bash scripts/create-bml-project.sh

Then in the board UI: View → Group by → Stage. Filter tip: label:experiment.

3. Wire Issues → Project

Create an experiment issue, then add it to the project:

gh issue create --title "Build Loop: …" --label experiment --body-file path/to/body.md
gh project item-add <PROJECT_NUMBER> --owner <ORG> --url <ISSUE_URL>

Or use New issue → Build Loop Experiment template (auto-applies experiment) and add the card to Backlog in the UI.

4. Ready to leave Backlog

  • One-sentence hypothesis
  • Kill criteria are numeric
  • Duration set
  • Measure path named (event IDs from metrics doc or “manual sheet”)

Abstract walkthrough

Context: A product team adopts Build Loop for the first time on a greenfield repo.

  1. They run gh label create for the four labels and confirm with gh label list.
  2. They execute create-bml-project.sh, open the board, and group by Stage.

  • Phase labels + columns — Card in Measure still tagged hypothesis. Pick one system; we use columns.
  • Board without issues — Cards that are not backed by an issue lose hypothesis and metrics. Always create the issue first.
  • Skipping Measure — Moving straight from Build to Learn because “we know it worked.” The clock and kill criteria exist for a reason.
  • Learn without a decision label — Vague comments on the issue. Apply exactly one of persevere, pivot, or kill-candidate.
  • Too many active builds — More than three experiments in Build+Measure for a small team. Finish measuring before starting more.

Module 4 · ~25 min

Ticket Template & Build Skills

The experiment issue template forces hypothesis-before-build discipline. In the Build column, a four-skill chain turns that issue into shipped, tested code — one tracer-bullet ticket at a time.

4

Why

Same shape every time; skills for non-trivial Build

~25 minutes

Experiments fail quietly when the ticket is vague: no kill criteria, no duration, no measure path. A fixed template makes the minimum bar obvious before anyone writes code.

Non-trivial Build work needs more than a template. The skill chain — grill-with-docs, to-spec, to-tickets, implement — sharpens the plan, publishes a spec, slices vertical tickets with blocking edges, then builds one ticket at a time with test-driven development and code review. Tiny builds can skip straight to implement.

Do not substitute to-issues plus bare tdd for this chain. implement owns tdd and code-review at the end of each ticket.

4

Micro-scenario

Abstract walkthrough

Context: Issue #40 proposes a guided checklist inside an onboarding flow. Build is multi-step (API + UI + analytics).

  1. PM files the template with hypothesis “users who see the checklist complete setup 20% more often,” kill at <5% lift, two-week measure window.
  2. Engineer runs grill-with-docs: one question at a time until “checklist item” vs “setup step” terms land in CONTEXT.md.
  3. to-spec publishes a spec: user stories, API contract decisions, test seams at the public onboarding API.
  4. to-tickets yields three tracer bullets: (1) API + empty UI shell, (2) checklist render + event, (3) dashboard readout — each blocked only by its predecessor.
  5. implement on ticket 1: tdd at the API seam, full suite once, code-review, commit. Context cleared; ticket 2 next.
  6. Card moves to Measure; no further skills until numbers arrive.

Failure modes

  • Full chain for a one-line fix — Adding analytics to an existing button does not need a spec and five tickets. Use implement.
  • Skipping grill on ambiguous scope — “Improve onboarding” without shared terms produces specs that rewrite themselves mid-build.
  • Horizontal tickets — “All API work” then “All UI work” cannot demo mid-flight. Use vertical tracer bullets.
  • Implementing multiple tickets in one context — Context rots; blockers blur. One ticket, then clear.
  • Bare tdd without implement’s review — Tests land without standards/spec check. Let implement run the full close-out.
  • Measure-phase feature creep — “While we wait, let’s also…” Invalidates the experiment clock.

5

Running an experiment

~30 min

Playbook

Walk one Build Loop experiment from Backlog through Done. Cursor helps on Build only. Humans own Measure discipline and Learn decisions.

← Module 4: Ticket template /tdd /code-review /prototype

Why run experiments end-to-end

A ticket that never reaches Learn taught you nothing — no matter how polished the Build was.

Most teams treat the board as a shipping pipeline. Build Loop treats it as a learning pipeline. Each experiment exists to falsify one customer-behavior claim within a fixed duration. The payoff is a recorded decision — persevere, pivot, or kill — backed by numbers, not narrative.

Running one experiment all the way through trains the habits that matter: thin Builds, frozen scope during Measure, and honest Learn outcomes. Skip any column and you get theater — activity that looks like learning but never changes what you build next.

Ideas to hold

  • One issue, one experiment. The GitHub issue is the source of truth; the project board is the visual phase tracker.
  • Build is the only Cursor-heavy column. Agents sharpen plans, publish specs, split tickets, and implement code. They do not decide whether the hypothesis passed.
  • Measure is a clock, not a build phase. Once the thinnest test ships and a collection path exists, the clock starts. No new feature scope until Learn closes the bet.
  • Learn is a decision, not a retrospective vibe. Apply one decision label and file one next action before moving to Done.
  • Kill is a success. Stopping a failed bet early is validated learning, not defeat.

How-to: five columns, one experiment

StageWhoWhat happensExit gate
Backlog Human Create issue from template. Write hypothesis, pass/kill thresholds, duration, sample size. Hypothesis + numeric kill criteria + duration written
Build Human + Cursor Ship the smallest test that lets Measure start. Use the skill chain from Module 4 when non-trivial. Delivery path exists and instrumentation or manual log path is named
Measure Human Collect weekly numbers into the issue. No code unless instrumentation is broken. Duration complete; metrics recorded on the issue
Learn Human Compare numbers to pass/kill. Record decision, evidence, next action. Apply one decision label. persevere, pivot, or kill-candidate applied
Done Human Archive when stale. Follow-up experiment filed if persevere or pivot. Next action exists or explicit “no follow-up” noted

Build column — Cursor workflow

Invoke skills only while the card is in Build. See Module 4 for the full chain and when to skip steps.

  1. /grill-with-docs — prime with the issue + relevant folders; sharpen assumptions and kill criteria
  2. /to-spec — publish a spec from the grilled conversation (skip if the Build fits one tiny PR)
  3. /to-tickets — split into vertical-slice tickets with blockers (skip if one ticket)
  4. /implement — one unblocked ticket at a time; runs /tdd at agreed seams, then /code-review, then commits

For design uncertainty before Build, use /prototype — throwaway code that answers “does this logic feel right?” or “what should this look?”

Agent boundary: Cursor helps on Build. Humans own Measure discipline and Learn decisions. An agent must not apply decision labels or declare a hypothesis validated.

Micro-scenario: weekly insight engagement test

Abstract pattern — swap the nouns for your product.

Hypothesis: Active customers will read and act on a recurring insight delivered each week.

Pass: ≥60% of sampled customers open the insight and complete at least one attributed action per week.

Kill: <40% engagement after four weeks.

Duration: 4 weeks · Sample: 20 active accounts

Backlog: Issue created from template. Measure table filled. Added to board.

Build: Ship email + in-app card for one insight type. Instrument delivered, opened, acted events (or a manual sheet with the same IDs). Cursor runs grill → spec → tickets → implement for the delivery path.

Measure: Every Monday, post open rate and act rate in the issue comment. No “while we’re at it” feature work.

Learn: Week 4 numbers land at 52% act rate — between pass and kill. Team decides pivot: shorten insight, add in-app-only channel, new 3-week experiment filed.

Metrics naming conventions: Metrics dictionary.

Failure modes

  • Build forever. Polishing the experiment until the duration is half gone. Gate: move to Measure when the thinnest path + collection method exist.
  • Measure creep. “Quick fix” during Measure that changes what you’re testing. File a new experiment instead.
  • Learn by vibes. Moving to Done with “customers seem happy” and no numbers on the issue.
  • Agent decides Learn. Letting Cursor apply persevere because the code merged. Humans compare metrics to the pass/kill table.
  • Vanity metrics. Counting logins or page views when the hypothesis is about action. Prefer outcome events defined before Build.
  • Skipping grill on risky bets. Building before kill criteria are sharp — you learn too slowly to decide.

  4. When the key uncertainty is design shape — “does this state model feel right?” or “what should this UI look?” — before committing to a production Build. Prototypes are throwaway; validated decisions fold into the real experiment issue.
6

Operating the board

~25 min

Playbook

Setup gets you a board. Operating keeps it honest: column gates, WIP limits, a weekly rhythm, and Learn outcomes that actually change what you build.

← Module 5: Running an experiment Kanban setup reference Implementation notes

Why operating rules exist

If Learn does not regularly produce kill, pivot, or persevere decisions, you are not running Build Loop — you are performing it.

A board without gates becomes a feature factory with extra columns. WIP limits force finish-over-start. Weekly cadence prevents cards rotting in Learn. Anti-theater rules exist because teams naturally optimize for shipping — not for stopping bad bets.

Operating discipline is how a small team runs multiple experiments without drowning in half-finished Builds or endless Measure phases with no decision.

Ideas to hold

  • Columns are phases, not suggestions. Each transition has a gate. Skipping a gate erases auditability.
  • Labels mark decisions, not phases. Only experiment plus Learn outcomes (persevere, pivot, kill-candidate). No phase labels — columns own phase.
  • The issue body holds evidence. Board cards are pointers; numbers and decisions live on the issue.
  • Three active maximum. Build + Measure combined — for a team of three, that is one experiment per person at most.
  • Learn clears before new Builds. If Learn is stuck, fix that before opening more experiments.

How-to: column gates

From → ToGate (all required)Common miss
Backlog → Build One-sentence hypothesis; numeric kill criteria; duration set “We’ll figure out metrics during Build”
Build → Measure Thinnest test shipped; instrumentation or manual log path named Moving when code merged but nothing can be counted yet
Measure → Learn Duration complete; weekly numbers recorded on the issue Moving early because “it looks good so far”
Learn → Done Decision label applied; evidence + one next action written Card moved to Done with no label or follow-up

How-to: 3-active WIP limit

Count every card in Build or Measure. Maximum three at once for a three-person team.

  1. Before pulling from Backlog, count active cards. At three? Finish or Learn something first.
  2. Measure counts as active — it consumes attention even when no code ships.
  3. Long-running Measure is not a license for parallel Builds. Either extend duration explicitly on the issue or move to Learn with an interim decision.
  4. Backlog can be deep. Active must stay shallow.

How-to: weekly cadence (~30 min total)

WhenRitualOutput
Monday Board review: unblock Measure; post weekly numbers; move anything past duration into Learn Every active experiment has fresh numbers or a named blocker
Wednesday Scope check: any silent Build creep on active cards? Creep either rejected or spun into a new Backlog experiment
Friday Learn sweep: every Learn card gets a decision or explicit defer with a date Zero orphan Learn cards without a plan

Learn-phase decisions

LabelMeaningTypical next action
persevere Hypothesis supported — continue or expand on current path Scale sample, deepen Build, or promote from experiment to product bet
pivot Signal mixed — change approach; core assumption may still hold New experiment issue with revised hypothesis and metrics
kill-candidate Hypothesis failed kill criteria — stop or sunset Archive feature path; document learning; do not quietly revive

Micro-scenario: stuck Learn queue

Abstract ops story — no product names.

Three experiments sit in Learn. Two have complete numbers; one is waiting on “one more week” for the third time. Meanwhile, Backlog has five eager ideas and someone wants to start two new Builds.

Anti-theater response:

  1. Friday Learn sweep: decide the two complete cards (one persevere, one kill-candidate).
  2. Defer card three with a date, or kill it — “waiting for perfect data” is not a decision.
  3. Hold new Builds until Learn count drops. Pull one Backlog card only after WIP drops below three.
  4. Post mortem note on the issue: “Learn queue stuck 3 weeks — weekly Friday sweep now mandatory.”

Week-1 Measure can be a spreadsheet. Event IDs should match the metrics dictionary so later instrumentation does not invent new names.

Failure modes — Learn anti-theater

  • Zero kills in a quarter. Either your kill criteria are too soft or Learn is being skipped. Fix Learn before more Builds.
  • Learn without numbers. Decisions based on memory or enthusiasm. Gate: metrics on the issue.
  • Perpetual Measure. Extending duration informally to avoid a kill. Update the issue or decide.
  • Board as wallpaper. Team ships from memory; cards do not move. Monday review is non-optional.
  • WIP blowout. Six active experiments, none reaching Learn. Enforce the three-active cap ruthlessly.
  • Label without action. pivot applied but no follow-up experiment filed. Done means done and next step exists.

Module 7 · ~30 min

Your first project

Ship a real experiment issue in sandbox — practice the full template without polluting the live board. A lead graduates it when the hypothesis and thresholds are sound.

7

Why

Sandbox before the live board

~30 minutes

Reading about the Build–Measure–Learn Loop is not the same as writing a falsifiable experiment ticket. Module 7 is the mandatory exercise: create your first real issue using the full template, with numeric pass and kill thresholds, a named measure path, and a duration.

Practice issues stay in sandbox until a lead promotes them. That protects the operating board’s WIP limit (max three active in Build+Measure combined) and keeps active experiments honest. Leads check template completeness and threshold quality before graduation — not whether you “like” the idea.

Hard truth: If your sandbox issue could not survive lead review, it should not have been on the live board yet.

7

How

Create, submit, graduate

Step 1 — Create the sandbox issue

  1. Open your product repo (or teaching repo for practice) → New issue → Build Loop Experiment template.
  2. Fill every section: hypothesis (one sentence), Build (smallest test), Measure table (pass + kill + how collected), duration, sample.
  3. Apply label experiment.
  4. Do not add the issue to the live Project board until a lead graduates it.

Step 2 — Post your submission

Share the issue URL where your lead specifies (repo Discussion, Slack, or team channel):

Build Loop Module 7 submission
Issue: https://github.com/YOUR-ORG/YOUR-REPO/issues/NNN
Hypothesis (one line): …

Step 3 — Lead graduation

Your lead reviews:

  • Complete template with falsifiable hypothesis
  • Numeric pass and kill criteria plus duration
  • Named measure path (instrumentation or manual log)
  • Fit under the three-active Build+Measure cap when promoted

When approved, the lead adds the issue to the live board at Backlog (or moves an existing draft). If sandbox-only practice is enough for your role, the lead confirms that on the certification checklist.

BookIQ teammates: create the sandbox issue on Book-IQ/bookiqv1-rc; lead graduates it to the Build Loop Experiments board when ready. See the BookIQ appendix for week-1 draft examples.

Failure modes

  • Live board pollution — adding practice tickets to the operating Project before review. Crowds WIP and trains the team to ignore Backlog discipline.
  • Vague hypothesis — “Improve onboarding” is not falsifiable. Name customer behavior you expect to see.
  • Missing kill criteria — without a numeric fail line, Learn becomes vibes-only theater.
  • Skipping submission — certification requires a posted link; silent sandbox issues do not count.
